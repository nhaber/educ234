Stanford University EDUC234/PSYCH240A

Syllabus and readings

Mondays, 3:00–5:50 PM (80 min + 10 min break + 80 min). All links checked March 2026.

Timing and readings are subject to change. Primary readings (bolded) are required; supplementary readings are not. For student presentations, students are welcome to suggest alternative readings at least a week in advance, though we reserve the right to insist on the assigned paper.

Two weeks prior to each student presentation, the instructor will provide suggested discussion questions to the presenting group. The group may modify these questions, and the final version will be announced to the class. Come prepared to discuss! The presentation group should make discussion a part of their presentation, which should be up to an hour, with slides.

Background

In this first section, we aim to provide a brief background in human development and artificial intelligence that will get us towards the study of sophisticated interactive learning and curiosity in artificial systems.

Week 1 — Monday, March 30
  • Part A: Introduction (80 min). Overall introduction, course goals, logistics.
  • — Break —
  • Part B: Developmental overview and world modeling (80 min). What do children learn early in life? How do we do so? Intuitive physics and psychology, early learning behaviors. What would it mean to engineer this sort of learning in artificial systems?
    Reading (all supplemental): A developmental approach to machine learning.
    From needs to goals and representations: Foundations for a unified theory of motivation, personality, and development.
Week 2 — Monday, April 6
  • Part A: Self-supervised learning and world models (80 min). How might we build artificial systems that can learn from their experience, as opposed to carefully-curated datasets?
    Reading: Self-Supervised Learning Methods for Computer Vision.
    Supplemental: Self-supervised learning, World Models (chiefly the blog posts).
  • — Break —
  • Part B: Introduction to reinforcement learning, part 1 (80 min). Key successes of the past decade, the theoretical framework, a brief overview of basic methods.
    Reading: Chapter 1 of HuggingFace's Deep RL course. (feel free to do more, but just worry about chapter 1) Participate in discussion on Canvas.
    Supplemental: Playing Atari with Deep Reinforcement Learning, Continuous Control with Deep Reinforcement Learning, Reinforcement Learning (book).
Week 3 — Monday, April 13
  • Part A: Introduction to reinforcement learning, part 2 (80 min). Benchmarks of the past decade. Current challenges.
    Reading: Huggingface's Interesting Environments to Try.
    Supplemental: Deep reinforcement learning that matters,
  • — Break —
  • Part B: Practicum — getting started with reinforcement learning (80 min). Hands-on session.
Week 4 — Monday, April 20
  • Part A: Project scoping pitches (80 min).
  • — Break —
  • Part B: Introduction to interactive learning (80 min). Classical active learning and optimal experiment design, a framework for interactive learning.
    Reading: Active Learning (just section 1).
    Supplemental: A Tutorial on Bayesian Optimization.

A deeper dive into world models

    Readings: Physion: Evaluating Physical Prediction from Vision in Humans and Machines. Learning to Simulate Complex Physics with Graph Networks.
    Supplemental: Simulation as an engine of physical scene understanding, Core knowledge, Interaction Networks for Learning about Objects, Relations and Physics, High Fidelity Video Prediction with Large Stochastic Recurrent Neural Networks.
  • — Break —
  • April 27, Student Presentation 2: Social world models (80 min).

Curiosity
